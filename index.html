The Power of Doing Nothing

Abstract

In modern society, most people have shifted their awareness to organizing lives around productivity and achievement instead of prioritizing well-being. As people increasingly value work, justifying the enjoyment of relaxation becomes challenging. Through psychological research on mental health, this paper attempts to provide an overview of cognitive shifts that counter inherent social propositions. This goes in hand with acts of rest, which question the traditional concept of doing nothing and social standards. The purpose of this thesis is to disclose a positive aspect of relaxation that is called doing nothing as a means of protective mechanism while living in a hustle culture.

I also could not have undertaken this journey without my family, who has kept my spirits and motivation high during this process. Thanks should also go to my friends who inspired me with late-night feedback sessions and enjoyment together of doing nothing. Lastly, I’d like to mention my dog for all the entertainment and emotional support.

Introduction

When was the last time you ‘did nothing’? A time when you could relax without thinking about your schedule or pending tasks. Perhaps it is a story from a distant past. “다음 생에는 돌로 태어나 아무것도 안 했으면 좋겠다.” Figure 1 The given sentence is a widely known expression about doing nothing in South Korea. It says in English, ‘I hope to be born as a stone and do nothing in my next life.’ Why did it become popular?

Korea, having experienced rapid growth in a short period, is a society where people tend to prioritize productivity. In my opinion, such growth made the organizational domain more important than the individual, leading people to focus on productivity and strategically adopt an interpretation of the majority. As a result, they may even be cautious about taking a brief break, fearing judgment from others. Therefore, the given sentence reflects the desire to sacrifice relaxation in this life to succeed and live well, with the hope of being able to rest in the next life.

I strived to achieve not only massive goals but also small particular tasks each day like washing dishes. However, if I feel trapped in to-do lists or consider every moment of the day as an opportunity to do more, this mindset can become problematic. Of course, I know how to do nothing. I can lie down, space out and waste time. But when I do these, my mind may be preoccupied with some kinds of tasks. The guilt of doing nothing prevents me from truly relaxing and enjoying nothing. This brought me to an exploration of the research question: “What is the meaning of doing nothing?”. I embarked on this journey to explore the aesthetics of ‘doing nothing’ and discover ways to navigate mental and physical well-being. In a productivity-obsessed culture, I believe it is essential that people explore the possibilities and value of ‘doing nothing’ to improve their lives, melt away stress and make themselves productive when they do work.

In the following chapters, I will delve into the various aspects of my thesis, starting with my experience of not wanting to do anything when I was in Korea. From there, I will explore the psychological impact of a productivity-oriented culture, how it induces cognitive dissonance, and ultimately the concept of doing nothing.

Moreover, when it comes to jobs, there is a tendency to value the degree of individuals’ efforts and achievements in seeking a job. Hustle Culture and Productivity Dysmorphia

“Why Can’t I See My Own Success?,” Anna Codrea-Rado, 2023. Productivity dysmorphia captures the feeling of not recognizing your achievements and constantly wanting to do more. Individuals grappling with productivity dysmorphia may feel a sense of shame and guilt because they perceive that they wasted time that they could have spent working.

Highly competitive and work-oriented cultures normalize the relentless pursuit of productivity, often at the expense of other life aspects. I often received praise when I prioritized productivity over well-being. When I interned at a Korean company, I was too busy to have lunch. Mental Exhaustion

We all encounter stress, which is the body's reaction to new, scary, or worrisome situations. Mental exhaustion, however, is a symptom of long-term stress. Consistently grappling with stress-inducing factors leads to a sustained elevation of specific hormone mixtures, eventually disrupting normal bodily functions such as digestion, sleep, the immune system, etc.

Mental exhaustion or “burnout” is a term commonly used to describe feeling overwhelmed or stressed, often related to work but potentially arising from prolonged stress in any facet of life. It can encompass work, family life, or a combination. In addition, physical fatigue, chronic illnesses, and injuries can contribute to mental exhaustion in individuals.

This is especially true when one is aware of experiencing a certain stress level early on and has appropriate mechanisms to overcome it.

Changes according to Cognitive Dissonance

When individuals experience the psychological stress of discomfort they undergo is referred to as “cognitive dissonance”, [22]
Cognitive dissonance is typically experienced as psychological stress of discomfort when persons experience a state of imbalance. The discomfort is triggered by the person’s beliefs conflicting with the other ones. they tend to change their cognition to resolve such discrepancies.Figure 4 [23]
Leon Festinger, “A Theory of Cognitive Dissonance,” 1957.

The theory proposed that to alleviate mental stress caused by cognitive dissonance, individuals seek psychological consistency through actions that diminish the conflict's intensity. This can involve adapting to, justifying against, or adopting an indifferent attitude toward the underlying contradiction causing mental distress. [24]
Festinger. In practical terms, consider a stressful situation in the hustle culture discussed earlier. Imagine you pledged to work harder in an achievement-oriented society. However, you find yourself not exerting any effort and achieving nothing. To reduce the cognitive dissonance, you may employ the strategies known as self-justification:

  1. Change the behaviour:
    I’ll work even harder without taking breaks.
  2. Justify the cognition:
    I’m allowed to cheat on my work in a while.
  3. Justify the cognition by adding new behaviour:
    Taking a break is acceptable.
  4. Ignore or deny information that conflicts with existing beliefs:
    Working hard is bad. And doing nothing is good.

I believe that self-compassion has an impact on self-justification. Self-compassion entails treating oneself with kindness during moments of pain or failure and maintaining a balanced awareness amidst challenging thoughts and emotions. It represents a warm and receptive attitude toward oneself, even in the aspects of life that one dislikes, and consists of three components. [25]
Kristin D. Neff, Stephanie S. Rude, and Kristin L. Kirkpatrick,
“An Examination of Self-Compassion in Relation to Positive Psychological Functioning and Personality Traits,” (2007):908-16. First, it includes being kind and understanding to oneself in situations of suffering or perceived inadequacy. It also involves a communal sense of humanity that recognizes that pain and failure are inevitable parts of human experiences. Lastly, self-compassion encompasses a balanced awareness of one's emotions — the ability to face distressing thoughts and feelings without exaggeration.

On the other hand, it is crucial not to be excessively lenient with self-compassion to the extent that it leads to self-sabotage. Susan Anderson states that “we sabotage ourselves in reaction to wounded and powerless early feelings”. [26]
Susan Anderson,
Taming Your Outer Child: Overcoming Self-Sabotage and Healing from Abandonment, (2015). For example, when I feel unintelligent, the anti-self [27]
Robert W. Firestone, Lisa Firestone, and Joyce Catlett,
The Self Under Siege: A Therapeutic Model for Differentiation, (2012). — the inner enemy formed a critical voice — adopts perspectives that reinforce how worthless I am. This internal critic sometimes became a nagging voice to ruin my plan and dismissed me as unworthy of doing whatever I wanted. I suppose that the core of self-sabotage is rooted in fear — fear of rejection, change, unknown, etc. These could trap me in procrastination, hindering progress toward my goals.

Resistance Mechanism as a Means of Control

I assume that most people aim to create positive and constructive changes, but they might come up against confronting their defeatist behaviours. Perhaps you have decided to study, work, and succeed even harder. However, you find yourself postponing or avoiding the things you knew would make your life better. In behavioural psychology, this phenomenon is called ‘resistance’. [28]
Lena M. Forsell and Jan A. Åström, “An Analysis of Resistance to Change Exposed in Individuals’ Thoughts and Behaviors,” (2012). It stems from a psychological response to change, akin to a self-protection mechanism that opposes alterations in one's preferred behaviour.

These mechanisms ultimately need to include appropriate resistant means of changing an individual's cognition, all while preventing the actor from falling into self-sabotage. A good example of this is the participatory performance in Korea known as the ‘Space-out Competition.’ Figure 5-1, 5-2 [29]
playlife, “[Playlife] 누구나 멍때릴 시간이 필요해요 - 작가 웁쓰양,” 2022. Initially, the competition posed the question to busy people, “Is doing nothing a waste of time?” The competition organizer, the artist Woopsyang said, “Many people continue to live hectic lives. A spacing-out competition was held with the idea: if you feel anxious about doing nothing alone, what if we all do it together?” When I heard about this competition at first, I thought the competition name sounded weird. However, the naming purpose intended to assign value to the activity of doing nothing by transforming it into a goal-oriented endeavour. Additionally, it seeks to create a visual contrast between the lives of busy people and the group that shamelessly does nothing. [30]
playlife, “[Playlife] 누구나 멍때릴 시간이 필요해요 - 작가 웁쓰양”.

It is not surprising that many people carry worries like “I'm struggling to manage my schedule,” “Stress is not released due to work,” and “I feel the need to do something even on my days off.” While living a stressful life, our well-being deteriorates. At this point, I would suggest adopting a fresh perspective that welcomes and accepts the act of ‘doing nothing.’ If there was a prejudice that doing nothing was considered a time-wasting activity in the past, the current perspective of it should be reinterpreted.

By building a method of “doing nothing” into your days, you can better balance your stress levels by leveraging your “resistance” reactions. These reactions entail achieving harmony between oneself and productivity by deliberately engaging in the practice of doing nothing. This approach may also assist you in establishing better boundaries between times of relaxation and concentration. Doing nothing is still considered to be something new and irregular. Does it work? For some, it does. For others, perhaps not. It all depends on the individual and whether they can find solace from the constant chaos of modern life through this method. In the upcoming chapter, I will introduce the aesthetics of ‘doing nothing’ and ways to incorporate it into your life so that you can practice it without guilt.

Seeking Balance through
Doing Nothing

In a productivity-oriented society, we might experience the harmful effects of productivity-driven mindsets on mental well-being. Those results can change an individual’s cognition in a worse way. In that context, it is crucial to recognize that the resulting mental struggles are problematic when they interfere with moments we want to focus on work or relaxation. As we need downtime to recharge and resume productivity, choosing to do nothing can signify achieving balance in our lives.

Rebranding of ‘Doing Nothing’

The concept of “doing nothing” is not entirely foreign to us. Similar terms or attitudes towards life can be found across various countries. First, there was la dolce far niente, an Italian expression meaning the sweetness of doing nothing, popularized by the movie “Eat Pray Love.” The Danish introduced us to hygge, which involves stepping away from the daily hustle to relax and enjoy quiet pleasures either alone or with loved ones. Then, Koreans have a concept called so-hwak-haeng,Figure 6-1, 6-2 which translates as finding small but certain happiness in life. There is another emerging trend to counteract busy and stressful lifestyles, niksen, which simply involves the concept of doing nothing in the Netherlands.

A sociologist Rutt Veenhoven, who is a director of the World Database of Happiness, said “The pace of life today is fast because there is much to enjoy.” He added, “Niksen provides a relief for people who feel overwhelmed by the pace. It is also an attractive idea for those who are not overwhelmed, but still feel the pressure, too.” [31]
Susan Devaney,
“What Is Niksen? The Dutch Lifestyle Concept That Allows Us To Do Nothing,” British Vogue, 2019. The benefit of doing nothing is that it can help generate new ideas. According to Veenhoven, “Even when we do nothing, our brain is still processing information and can use the available processing power to solve pending problems.” [32]
Sophia Gottfried,
“Niksen Is the Dutch Lifestyle Concept of Doing Nothing—And You’re About to See It Everywhere,” TIME, 2019

“If you remain focused all the time — constantly doing, doing, doing — you’re not allowing yourself into that mode that will actually lead to the greatest creativity that you have.”
by Emma Seppälä [33]

The term “work-life balance” is commonly discussed about managing work and personal time. Initially, I misunderstood it as a mockery of relaxation. Because it denotes an imbalance favouring work over personal life from the negative connotations in Korea. Many mistakenly perceive work and life as distinct entities that should be balanced equally. How to ‘Do Nothing’

Practicing doing nothing involves simply idling without any productive intent or purpose for a brief period. It may look easy, but it is much more challenging. There is no one-size-fits-all advice for learning how to do nothing, but here are some ways to make it more comfortable:

Primarily, addressing feelings of shame that some individuals may experience when not being highly productive or when taking breaks is essential. Chris Bailey, a productivity expert, claims that “we feel guilty when our actions don’t align with our values and that a solution to the guilt could be to learn to value doing nothing and relaxation.” [36]
Olga Mecking, “Do something good for yourself — nothing,” interview by Lisa Selin Davis, 2021. If you have trouble relaxing because you fear disappointing others or yourself, consider the kind of presence you want to offer to them. Making space for self-care allows you to be helpful. It is not about redefining yourself in a positive light compared to being negative; it allows you to reframe it as unproductive, half-hearted or any other negative adjective. This process is tough, and negative emotions may pop out. Instead of fighting or ignoring them, I recommend embracing tough things. Allowing these emotions to exist is not only crucial for dealing with them but also for finding comfort. Awareness of “negative space” [37]
The concept of negative space signifies in medicine what is not seen, not heard, not felt, or otherwise not done or experienced. [38]
Stephen A. Buetow, “Something in Nothing: Negative Space in the Clinician-Patient Relationship,” (2009). helps to open new possibilities. Most people consume cognitive resources and skills to interpret and judge the “positive space” of thoughts. But what about the opposite? I believe negative spaces in our thoughts can also exist. Thinking about it can open a new understanding of well-being and reveal opportunities that individuals control.

Once you have accepted negative spaces in your thoughts, it is time to create “cracks in time” in your life patterns and behaviours. Most people commute and work simultaneously every day, making it difficult for the brain to receive new stimuli. [39]
가토 도시노리, 아무것도 하기 싫은 사람을 위한 뇌 과학. To increase stimuli, you need to change your lifestyle pattern. For instance, changing the routine of starting the day, such as jogging or meditating before heading to work, can be beneficial. Alternatively, even if you do the same tasks, setting specific start and end times can be efficient for you. When tasks are allocated specific time slots, it fosters concentration (boosting arousal levels), thereby enabling the brain and body to handle a substantial workload throughout the day. Meanwhile, it can be overwhelming for a lethargic person to suddenly increase their level of activity. In such cases, a possible approach is to change the position of a desk. The position or arrangement of furniture and other items generally remains unchanged for prolonged periods in homes. Occasional changes in location or structure can increase efficiency by shortening the movement or bringing about a change in mood. If you change the desk position, you should rearrange the other items accordingly. It will help keep your brain active at a high level if this series of tasks can create new desires or spark other interests.

These acts of doing nothing can be done anytime, anywhere, and even alone. There is no need for unnecessary effort or preparation; it can be seamlessly integrated into our busy daily lives. Perhaps ‘Doing nothing’ feels uncomfortable until you get used to it. Therefore, practice is necessary, and as you become accustomed to it and it becomes a habit, you will gradually feel good about it. It is an important life skill to find the balance between busyness and rest.

Conclusion

The inception of my thesis stemmed from a personal question: “What is the meaning of doing nothing?” This question was sparked by my experiences within a productivity-oriented society. Under the pressure to constantly achieve, I found myself grappling with stress and difficulty in relaxing. Through observing the pervasive influence of productivity dysmorphia, I noted that individuals often feel guilty about taking breaks, even when they have completed sufficient work. As society increasingly glorifies work, individuals may consequently experience heightened levels of stress and mental exhaustion. However, with adequate coping mechanisms in place, this burden can be alleviated. In my research journey, I encountered numerous psychological experts who researched the correlation between stress and cognitive changes. Their studies revealed that individuals undergo cognitive dissonance when their personal beliefs conflict with social propositions, leading to behaviours such as self-justification, self-compassion, and self-sabotage. To prevent them from falling into the danger of self-sabotage, there is a need for means to change individuals’ cognition so that they can resist social propositions appropriately. My proposal advocates for a reinterpretation and acceptance of the concept of doing nothing. This entails incorporating intervals of slight change within a busy day. Doing nothing is not about something new; rather, it involves striking a balance between busyness and rest by modifying existing tasks.
